BioElectronics Group


TL;DR:
• Built a treadmill and trained rats and mice in order to investigate the recovery of locomotor function after spinal cord injury
• Aided in the fabrication of custom neural probes
• Implemented a cloud-hosted neural network to perform motion capture video analysis


My project in the Bioelectronics Group, evolved over a couple years and involving a great host of subprojects. The overarching project investigated the potential for light to induce neuro-regeneration in rat models of spinal cord injury. I was initially recruited to design and build a rat treadmill for assessing the recovery of locomotor function. I was responsible for the entire development process, from background research, to design, fabrication, and testing. I created CAD models and fabricated my designs with a laser cutter and lathe. I also sourced and tested motors and elastic belt materials to optimize cost and performance. I iterated on the treadmill multiple times based on testing results. For example, in one early version of the treadmill, the belt was prone to slipping off of its track. Through researching related fields, I was able to identify and implement a solution - creating a convex surface for the belt pulley to generate differential tensions resulting in a self-centering system. This experience was great practice in the engineering design process, from ideation to fabrication and iteration.

It was my good fortune to get to work with the incredible Dr. Polina Anikeeva and to have a most talented and thoughtful mentor, Dr. Dena Shahriari, who, recognizing my enthusiasm for learning, offered further involvement in the incredibly multidisciplinary project. In addition to engaging in mechanical engineering and design, I gained exposure to methods for developing implantable electronics and neural probes as well as for animal handling and testing. When tasked with introducing a procedure for generating motion data from videos of the treadmill use, I taught myself computer vision methods and developed programs to conduct basic analysis. I ultimately identified a more advanced open-source platform, and through much trial and error figured out how to harness this tool for our application by training and implementing a cloud-based neural network to process the raw video data. Through this sub-project, I gained experience in integrating electronics and data processing.

In addition to technical skills, I learned more general truths about the research process. The focus and design of the project were adapted numerous times as a partner neurosurgeon had to leave the project (requiring a new partner be found and brought up to date) and the surgical procedures had to be repeatedly modified to reduce the rate of casualties. Surgical and unexpected casualties were especially catastrophic setbacks, as each new cohort of rats set us back by months. I have learned patience and an appreciation for all the discovery that goes unpublished in the process of enabling that which is ultimately reported. Now as I enter my senior year, Dena has acquired a much deserved faculty position at the University of British Columbia and the project will leave MIT with her. The experiences I have had through this project have added tools to my engineering toolbox and provided valuable lessons in persistence and adaptability in the face of obstacles.

Newman Lab


TL;DR:
I Used LabView to interface with a therapeutic robot’s FPGA controller and adapt the robot to provide a platform for investigating auditory feedback in control of fine locomotor functions.


My freshman year I managed to get a position in the Newman Biomechanics and Rehabilitation Lab. In this project I adapted a user-machine interface program to process motion parameters into auditory output for an investigation into feedback-mediated locomotor control.

To do this I had to first learn LabView - a software used to adapt the robot's FPGA controller. The investigation of auditory feedback was inspired in part by the motor control of musicians so, after developing the needed coding skills and adapting the feedback program, I created CAD models and 3D printed handle adaptations so users could grip the robot handle like a violin bow.

I then collected some initial data, analyzed my findings, and presented to a joint lab meeting with collaborators at Northeastern University.

My time in the Newman Lab served as a gateway to my future research experiences at MIT and guided my understanding of my own interests.

ECMO

ECMO stands for Extracorporeal Membrane Oxygenator Circuit. ECMO can provide life-saving support of heart and lung function by circulating the blood outside the body, where carbon dioxide is removed and oxygen is reintroduced before the blood is pumped back into the body. In this project I explored aspects of ECMO design that can contribute to the formation of life-threatening blood clots (thrombosis). I worked with a team of peers to conduct an extensive literature review and identified the turbulance at the ECMO pump and stagnance at the membrane oxygenator, as well as the bio-incompatibility of tubing surfaces, to be factors contributing to clot formation within the circuit. I then investigated the potential to improve the detection of clots forming in the circuit. Towards this end I reached out to international scientists to learn more about promising recent advances, consulted local physicians, and was even able to visit an ECMO ward and gain insight from nurses at Massachusetts General Hospital. After gaining an understanding of current detection methods and limitations, I set about exploring and testing alternative detection strategies. Based on preliminary data on the thermal dynamics of clotting, I first conducted tests with an IR camera to assess the feasiblity of clot detection with thermal imaging. While this method would be optimal in its practicality, my suspicions were confirmed as energy delta for clotting did not provide a detectable signal. As the pandemic limited further testing of alternative strategies, my collegues and I have refocused our efforts on other pursuits for now.

Laryngoscopes

The airways of brachycephalic dogs and patients of all species with anatomical abnormalities such as protruding tumors can limit the ability to breath normally. These abnormal airways can make emergency intubation especially difficult. Currently, a tool called a laryngoscope is used to displace structures ventrally to enable intubation. In this project I designed modifications to existing laryngoscopes in order to aid in difficult endotracheal intubations by allowing displacement of swollen tissues and structures in multiple directions. The project is currently on hold for the duration of the global pandemic.
